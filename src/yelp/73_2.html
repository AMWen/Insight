I came to St. Peter's for one of the few happy reasons to be at a hospital - to have a baby. I was induced for medical reasons. I showed up at the hospital at 6 am and was checked into the labor and delivery area. By 6:30 am I was set up in a labor room and by 7:00 am the induction was underway. Between 7:00 am and 7:00 pm, I had a wonderful labor nurse who was in the room more often than not. At 7:00 pm, they switched nurse shifts (they're on for 12 hours), and I had 2 nurses for the evening who were in the room until I delivered at 2:00 am and then were in and out until they moved me to the postpartum wing at around 6:00 am. I know there were also several nurses in the room for the baby when she arrived and I did see two anesthesiologists during my time in labor and delivery. Everyone I encountered was wonderful and patient and went beyond my expectations. Once in postpartum, I saw several nurses, techs, and doctors. All were friendly and pleasant, even when seeing me at 3-4 am when exhausted from newborn feedings. The security measures in the hospital were great for the newborns and everyone made sure to explain procedures and even the nurses we were familiar with were not bothered when we asked to check to make sure they were authorized to take the baby to the nursery (in the wee hours of the morning it's hard to keep track). They were patient when I had questions and needed things. The lactation consultant came and saw me at least once a day and several other members of the hospital came by and checked on me during my stay - a financial person to discuss insurance payments, a social worker to answer any questions, a psychologist to make sure *I* was doing okay after birth (they check on mommys a lot to make sure you are handling any baby blues or postpartum issues okay), a religious officiant came by to offer to bless the baby or as someone to talk to if we needed (it is a religious affiliated hospital after all and he was very pleasant when I declined and told him we were not religious people). While we were there, our sweet little girl got moved to the NICU. The NICU nurses and doctors were outstanding. While we were there for "observation" and some testing, they made sure we were always fully informed of what was going on. I was breastfeeding and they were great about letting me go back to my room to do things like change outfits and sleep and calling me when she was hungry. Also, the postpartum nurses were great since I was rarely in my room for them to check on me and when I did return to my room, I frequently wanted things (like meds or supplies) quick and to get back to the NICU. Everyone was super understanding and willing to do whatever it took to make sure both the baby and I were being taken care of and able to bond and spend as much time together as possible. Thankfully, we were out of the NICU in a few days, but the short time we spent there was great. My only complaint about my stay in the hospital was the food service. My husband and I were frequently ordering the "room service" and not once did we get something where the order wasn't messed up. Sometimes they forgot little things (like tomato on a burger) and sometimes they forgot big things (like one of our meals or a side with the meal or silverware). The food was tolerable - not amazing, but also wasn't so bad we couldn't eat it. Other than that, the rooms were great and spacious and well cleaned and our stay in the hospital was wonderful. I can't say I necessarily want to come back again, but if I had to pick a hospital for something, I'd highly consider returning to St. Peter's.

    A hospital is a miserable place to be sick.  Saint Peter's does their best to make it bearable, even pleasant.

    Mom was rushed in almost three weeks ago, with a staggeringly high heart rate and blood pressure so low it almost didn't register.  How she stayed alive, let alone vertical is a testament to her stubbornness and Irish resolve to let nothing slow her down.

    After staving off the life-threatening atrial fibrillation, Mom's stay on 5 McCauley became a daily chess match to craft a complex pharmacopeia that would manage her heart rate while she fought through bronchitis and the impaired lung function that comes from the lung cancer she's living with.  Managing these complex drug levels and interactions was the work of a team of doctors led by the unflappable Dr. Nini C. Wu, her Oncologist, but especially by a team of nurses and technicians who monitored Mom at 4 hour intervals and made complicated medication adjustments as needed.  To an individual, each member of the staff, from vital signs techs to dietitians to housekeeping to nurses, respiratory therapists, phlebotomists and physical/occupational therapists, was pleasant, respectful and professional, and each interaction was remarkably friendly and helpful.

    Mom is a patient patient, far from demanding and fully compliant with all medical directives, but she is also naturally inquisitive, and since her cancer diagnosis has become a walking medical reference book on the various meds she has taken or is taking.  Her questions, double-checks and follow-ups were taken seriously and addressed fully, and at no time was she made to feel like she had been bothersome or a problem for asking so many questions.

    We are a large family, and a large circle of friends surrounds Mom as well, and the 5th floor staff took that in stride as well.  Our frequent visits, filling the small double room and our frequent questions were also handled with courtesy and with reliable information conveyed.  Even the historically bad hospital meals were not to be found--Mom's meals were flavorful, served hot and well-prepared, with nice presentation even.  (One of the dietary order takers was a bit brusque at times, but he was a glaring exception).

    Getting out of the hospital took far longer than we had expected, and Mom was disappointed several times as she approached what she thought would be her release date.  Every time, the nursing and tech staff would cheer her up, and let her know that they all had her best interests at heart and wanted to make sure she was "tip top" before release.  Today, one day shy of 3 weeks, Mom was sprung.  Now the home healing begins.

    Thanks St. Peter's, NY Oncology, and especially the entire staff on 5 McCauley for making something typically unpleasant as pleasant as a hospital stay can be.
