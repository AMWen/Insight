Writing this review was a little harder than I thought it would be, due to the mixed experience I had at BI. I'll try my best, though. A few weeks ago I had some serious back pain and a fever of 103 on top of a cold. I figured that it would be a good idea to go to the ER and get it checked out. I'm a pretty healthy individual, and while I wasn't sure what hospital to go to, I was also grateful that in my 2 years I've spent in Boston I haven't NEEDED to make that decision. However, there's a first time for everything, I suppose. While navigating through Longwood I realized the weird dichotomy of huge hospitals. On the one hand, I'm grateful that we have huge, impressive, modern, state-of-the-art medical/teaching facilities. On the other hand, it sure is a bitch to navigate around those ginormous campuses when you're sick and feeble in the middle of winter. I digress. I eventually found my way to Beth Israel's ER and checked in immediately. Once I was registered, I had about a 5 minute wait until I was shown to a room. Score. It probably helped that I was there on a Sunday morning the weekend before New Year's, when everyone is away on vacation and/or sleeping off hangovers. So round one of my hospital visit: I'm told that I have a UTI and I'm given a three-day Rx for Bactrim. I head over to CVS to fill out, where I'm told that the Rx is incorrect. The pharmacist has to call the attending physician who saw me at BI and change the Rx over the phone. Annoying, but at least someone was looking out for me. Boo for doctor error, though. Round two: I'm still feeling feverish and in a lot of pain, so I go back over to BI. This time they test my blood and hook me up to an IV. Oh, joy--turns out I have pyelonephritis (a kidney infection)! Fabulous. This time I'm given Ciprofloxacin (which is the stuff they give you if you've been exposed to anthrax). They don't kid around. More bed rest and hydration ensues. Round three: I'm still highly feverish and in pain. Back to the ER I go, where they decide to keep me overnight (after saying that they might have to send me home since it's my third visit in a row) and pump me full of IVs all night long. The nurses do a good job of hydrating and checking up on me, which is obviously a plus. I finally recover. So here are the pros and cons of my stay @ BI: Pros: -Fast service. Every day that I was in the ER I was registered and seen within about 10 minutes of arrival. -Close to home. -CVS, where I get my regular prescriptions, is right down the street for uber-convenience in filling a new prescription. There's also a Walgreens nearby. Cons: -Didn't get my blood tested right away, which necessitated subsequent visits for an upgrade in antibiotics. -This isn't unique to BI, but rather the hospital experience itself. I hate hospitals. I hate the long waits in between getting examined and poked and prodded by various medical professionals. I hate the exam rooms. I hate the stupid chafe-y johnnies that are required for everyone and flatter no one. I hate being stuck next to a skeletal ancient old lady who won't stop moaning at midnight when I'm trying to sleep. -The doctor messed up my Bactrim prescription. Honest mistake, I know, but I don't want to spend an extra hour in CVS, battling a fever and back pain while waiting for the pharmacist to get a hold of him to change it. -Confusing directions on the website to get to the ER. Boooooo. Overall, though, I had a pretty positive experience at BI. I'd come back in the future, but hopefully I won't need to visit a hospital again for a very, very long time. A ta sante!

    Writing this review was a little harder than I thought it would be, due to the mixed experience I had at BI. I'll try my best, though.

    A few weeks ago I had some serious back pain and a fever of 103 on top of a cold. I figured that it would be a good idea to go to the ER and get it checked out. I'm a pretty healthy individual, and while I wasn't sure what hospital to go to, I was also grateful that in my 2 years I've spent in Boston I haven't NEEDED to make that decision. However, there's a first time for everything, I suppose. Luckily for me I live near Longwood Medical Area,  which is probably the one area of Boston in which I'd be delighted to collapse on the street.

    While navigating through Longwood I realized the weird dichotomy of huge hospitals. On the one hand, I'm grateful that we have huge, impressive, modern, state-of-the-art medical/teaching facilities. On the other hand, it sure is a bitch to navigate around those ginormous campuses when you're sick and feeble in the middle of winter.

    I digress. I eventually found my way to Beth Israel's ER and checked in immediately. Once I was registered, I had about a 5 minute wait until I was shown to a room. Score. It probably helped that I was there on a Sunday morning the weekend before New Year's, when everyone is away on vacation and/or sleeping off hangovers.

    So round one of my hospital visit: I'm told that I have a UTI and I'm given a three-day Rx for Bactrim. I head over to CVS to fill out, where I'm told that the Rx is incorrect. The pharmacist has to call the attending physician who saw me at BI and change the Rx over the phone. Annoying, but at least someone was looking out for me. Boo for doctor error, though.

    Round two: I'm still feeling feverish and in a lot of pain, so I go back over to BI. This time they test my blood and hook me up to an IV. Oh, joy--turns out I have pyelonephritis (a kidney infection)! Fabulous. This time I'm given Ciprofloxacin (which is the stuff they give you if you've been exposed to anthrax). They don't kid around. More bed rest and hydration ensues.

    Round three: I'm still highly feverish and in pain. Back to the ER I go, where they decide to keep me overnight (after saying that they might have to send me home since it's my third visit in a row) and pump me full of IVs all night long. The nurses do a good job of hydrating and checking up on me, which is obviously a plus. I finally recover.

    So here are the pros and cons of my stay @ BI:

    Pros:
    -Fast service. Every day that I was in the ER I was registered and seen within about 10 minutes of arrival.
    -Close to home.
    -CVS, where I get my regular prescriptions, is right down the street for uber-convenience in filling a new prescription. There's also a Walgreens nearby.

    Cons:
    -Didn't get my blood tested right away, which necessitated subsequent visits for an upgrade in antibiotics.
    -This isn't unique to BI, but rather the hospital experience itself. I hate hospitals. I hate the long waits in between getting examined and poked and prodded by various medical professionals. I hate the exam rooms. I hate the stupid chafe-y johnnies that are required for everyone and flatter no one. I hate being stuck next to a skeletal ancient old lady who won't stop moaning at midnight when I'm trying to sleep.
    -The doctor messed up my Bactrim prescription. Honest mistake, I know, but I don't want to spend an extra hour in CVS, battling a fever and back pain while waiting for the pharmacist to get a hold of him to change it.
    -Confusing directions on the website to get to the ER. Boooooo.

    Overall, though, I had a pretty positive experience at BI. I'd come back in the future, but hopefully I won't need to visit a hospital again for a very, very long time. A ta sante!
