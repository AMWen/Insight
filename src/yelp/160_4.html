    I had a stroke of misfortune that led to me needing ACL reconstruction surgery.

    I play roller derby and injured my knee during a local scrimmage. Very shortly after I learned I would need surgery to repair my ACL, my nurse friend (who also plays roller derby) texted me and said that I should really check and see if my insurance would allow me to see someone at the Hospital for Special Surgery.

    I had not heard of HSS before, but I started to hear the name everywhere as I talked to people about my injury and my need for orthopedic surgery. I actually work at Columbia University Medical Center (CUMC) and everyone there strongly urged me to check out HSS as well.

    I checked with my insurance (Aetna), and lo and behold, they would allow me to see someone at the Hospital for Special Surgery.

    I settled on Dr. Andrew Pearle, who had reconstructed the ACL of another one of my roller derby teammates. She is retired now, but she did return to skating after her surgery, so I figured that was a good sign. I was able to get in for surgery very quickly (I think Dr. Pearle had a cancellation). I scheduled my knee surgery for a Friday morning a mere 3 days after first seeing Dr. Pearle.

    My husband accompanied me the day of the procedure. We had to park in a super expensive parking lot on the UES.

    The waiting area was very comfortable. There were also signs asking you to please not eat. This struck me as very considerate since I had not eaten since the night before and was STARVING; the last thing I needed was a bunch of people eating bagels or something near me.

    Eventually I was called to do some paperwork and then shortly after that I was called back to start getting ready for surgery.

    The nurses who did all of my prep work were very nice. A physical therapist also came in to show me how my brace worked and how to walk on crutches. My anesthesiologist came in to talk about what kind of sedation I would like. I was nervous but they all did their best to make very normal conversation with me. I can't say it helped me feel more relaxed, but I know they were trying.

    After a lot of prep, and one last trip to the bathroom (it's really hard to pee with an IV in your hand!), I walked myself into the operating room (SO WEIRD!) and chatted with my anesthesiologist for a bit. And then he put me under, and the next thing I remember is being wheeled into the recovery room.

    I sat in recovery for a long time while the feeling came back to my legs. I was given a sandwich which I swear was the most delicious thing I have ever eaten in my life.

    During this time there was a miscommunication where none of the nurses ever went to get my husband from the big waiting area to say he could come back to visit with me. So I was by myself for a few hours and also thinking that my husband may have left the building for some reason unbeknownst to me...??? But someone eventually realized no one had ever alerted him and then he was able to come back and see me -- but only in 20-minute increments.

    After a few hours in recovery, I had to prove I could use the bathroom on my own, and then they helped me get dressed in my own clothes and it was time to go! And by this time it was 4:50pm and my husband had to drive me home to Astoria from Manhattan during rush hour on a Friday. SUPER FUN.

    Really this is a five star place and if you need orthopedic surgery, I highly recommend HSS. I am subtracting one star for the miscommunication in the recovery room that caused me to panic and think that my husband had possibly abandoned me.

History

Established in 1863.

Founded in 1863, Hospital for Special Surgery is the nation's oldest orthopedic hospital. More than 25,000 surgical procedures are performed annually. HSS performs more hip surgeries and more knee replacements than any other hospital in the nation. HSS surgeons who specialize in joint replacement perform, on average, 365 of these operations every year.
